As of May 1944, the Canadian Army had not fought a major armoured en-

combat experience gained in Italy remained in Italy, as none of the ist Canadian

As a result, by spring 1944 Canadian armoured regiments in the United
Kingdom had no combat experience advancing as the offensive vanguard. When
this was attempted in Normandy, German antitank fire dealt large casualties
to Canadian armoured squadrons equipped with inferior technology.?

During the later stages of August 1944's Operations Totalize and Tractable,
the armoured regiments of the 4th Canadian Armoured Brigade were asked
to manoeuvre rapidly in a fluid situation that demanded aggression and initia-
tive from all squadron commanders." The operations were temporarily blocked
by weak German armoured forces and held up by traffic jams. The brigade's
performance was lacklustre because of inexperience and a large number of of-
ficer casualties. During Operation Totalize, when fighting near Rocquancourt-
Cintheaux on 9 August, several 22nd Canadian Armoured Regiment command
tanks were destroyed. The loss of a squadron commander and two troop leaders
at the forefront of the battle significantly degraded command and control 18
This newly deployed formation was fighting its first battles in August at the
vanguard of the First Canadian Army's advance. To ensure success, the Canadian
Army needed an abundance of experienced armoured regiments in this role,
not ones carrying out their first combat operations."
Without intimate artillery and infantry support, Canadian tanks were vulner-
able to all forms of antitank weapons. Canadian Sherman 75 mm and 17-pounder
Sherman Firefly variants were susceptible to high-velocity German armour-
piercing antitank rounds. The Sherman's armoured plate was not angled enough,
hardened enough, or thick enough in its frontal areas. Operational research
reports found it took on average 1.63 hits to destroy or knock out a Sherman;
91.2 percent of Shermans with gasoline engines quickly burned out? When
German fire hit the internal hull shell racks, it caused catastrophic explosions.
Also, the Sherman's profile was too high, and it lacked a 360-degree episcope
cupola that the commander could see out of without exposing his head."
