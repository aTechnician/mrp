The use of conscription in Canada during the First World War had had a large
political impact on the nation. In August 1917, Prime Minister Robert Bordens
government enacted the Military Service Act, which aimed to maintain the
strength of the Canadian Corps at a time when it was facing a severe shortage
of recruits, regardless of success at the front. Over 45,000 personnel conscripted
from 1 January 1918 onward reached the front lines by war's end.* These men
were taken directly from their civilian environment and placed in military
training camps. The cost of this decision was national disunity along ethnic
lines, with anglophone Canadians being overwhelming proconscription and
French Canadians vehemently opposed. Unrest resulted in riots and numerous
revolutionary acts in Quebec. Politically, it damaged the Liberal Party's stand-
ings in English Canada, reducing Wilfrid Lauriers federal Liberal Party to seats
largely within Ouebec after the election of 1017 In 1020. King's Liberal governo
ment viewed even the proposed use of conscription as a serious matter.

When King was forced by military necessity to reverse his decision in fall
1944, the damage to national unity was not as great. The servicemen sent were
NRMA personnel who had been in the army in some cases for several years
instead of new recruits violently stripped from the civilian population. Civilian
men who were not in the army in late 1944 did not have their lives affected by
this decision. This factor was extremely influential in reducing negative political
reaction within Quebec. Furthermore, in 1942 King had put the issue of con-
scription to the Canadian public in a plebiscite, and voters had overwhelmingly
endorsed the idea, giving the government a level of political cover.
Army growth from 1941 to 1944 occurred against a backdrop of political in-
fighting over the issue of conscription. This battle pitted the proconscription
minister of national defence, J.L. Ralston, against the anticonscriptionist forces
within the War Committee, most notably the prime minister and Minister of
Mines and Resources T.A. Crerar. Those in the minister's camp consistently
held onto the main goal of resisting conscription to achieve three main goals:
retain the Liberal Party's hold on power and political fortunes; avoid nationwide
unrest over conscription; and maintain Canada's contribution to the war effort
at a manageable minimum level. This was made clear by the War Committee's
29 July 1941 rejection of the army's requests to increase its size by a full two
divisions. 10
