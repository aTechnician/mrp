During the brief halt in Dieppe in early September, reinforcements equivalent
to eight new infantry companies reached the and Canadian Infantry Divisions
infantry regiments. Further transfers continued all month. Many of these men
had been remustered from other trades. This compromise solution to the short-
age of readily available other ranks infantry utilized personnel already in the
reinforcement stream to increase the speed in which they would reach their
new units. These personnel often did not have a high level of infantry training
prior to their remustering, and the transformation of a Royal Canadian Artillery
(RCA) antiaircraft gunner to an infantryman could not be accomplished in-
stantaneously. Luckily for the division, combat operations north of Antwerp
did not begin until earty October 1944. Thus by the beginning of the Battle of
the Scheld - operations to clear the approaches to the vital deepwater port of
Antwerp - reinforcements accepted early in the month of September had had
some time to train and acclimatize themselves.

With its new strength, the division plunged into combat operations in eastern
Belgium and the southern Netherlands in fall 1944. In late September, initial
operations had begun to clear the area west of Antwerp in preparation for a
drive north on the left flank of the 4th Canadian Armoured Division. The div-
ision crossed the Antwerp- Turnhout Canal on 22 September and regrouped for
an advance north. The eventual goal was clearing the northern bank of the
Scheldt Estuary to free the port of Antwerp.
