Following the successful reconstitution of all its infantry regiments by Octo-
ber 1944, the next hurdle that the Canadian Army (Active) had to jump to
stabilize its infantry personnel situation was the creation of a sizable reserve
of reinforcements in the United Kingdom and Belgium. This reserve would be
maintained so that all ongoing and future infantry attrition could be serviced.
Despite the large number of remusters, in 30 November the army still had
trouble creating this reserve. As soon as infantry personnel arrived in the re-
inforcement system they were being dispatched to the regiments. This resulted
in a gulf between the authorized reinforcement strength and the actual total
reinforcements on hand. Figure 2.4 illustrates the challenge the Canadian Army
faced.
When the shortage ended, the Conscription Crisis began in Ottawa. In Octo-
ber 1944, the minister of national defence returned from visiting the Canadian
Army in the Netherlands and Italy. Meetings during his travels had raised the
concern that if infantry casualties in northwest Europe continued at their cur-
rent pace or if a large military event occurred, the available reinforcement pools
could be extinguished by the end of the year.?" His concerns were based on
uncertainty within the army about whether an adequate reserve could be cre-
ated from volunteer Canadian Army (Active) personnel? On 13 October,
CMHQ Chief of Staff Lieutenant-General Stuart informed the chief of the
general staff in Ottawa that 60,000 to 40,000 more losses could be expected
in the coming months. Stuart made the immediate request for 16,000 more men.

6,000 over the succeeding months?" In the end, only a small number reached
