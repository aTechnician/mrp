This transformation put the final administrative and command touches on
the maintenance process within a fully mechanized Canadian Army. The system
was now more capable than ever of keeping a major percentage of vehicles
and major weapons systems operational. The creation of an entirely new army
corps and the adoption of new repair-and-recovery processes were significant
mileposts in the evolution of the Canadian Army during the Second World
War. Overcoming extreme institutional resistance, former RCOC "E" Branch
officers had finally prevailed.
When conducting operations, the Canadian Army's recovery-and-repair sys-
tem was divided into levels so that no one area would be overstressed by its
workload. If there was an overload at one level or if a task was too complicated,
tasks would be passed to the next successive level, commonly designated as a
line. As illustrated in Figures 3.6 and 3.7, RCEME had four lines to repair mech-
anical faults or battle damage. First line was the organic repair services attached
to the combat arms regiments. The lines that followed took on successively more
