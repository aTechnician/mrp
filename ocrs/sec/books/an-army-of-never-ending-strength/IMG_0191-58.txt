Corps- and army-level combat arms units under the command of the First
Canadian Army headquarters and the ist and znd Canadian Corps were in-
dependent of Canadian divisions and independent brigades. These units were
made available to corps and divisional commanders, who could attach them to
their formations on a need basis. These units could increase the combat power
of a division during offensive operations or strengthen its defensive front. Most
of them were part of RCA, and their indirect firepower was utilized to help
implement the artillery-based doctrine of the Canadian Army in the late war
period.
The higher command structures that allocated these units included the First
Canadian Army headquarters, which was formed in April 1942 when the number
of Canadian divisions in the United Kingdom reached the strength of two corps.
It became operational in Normandy on 31 July 1944, and the army's commander-
for the duration of the campaign in northwest Europe was General H.D.G.
Crerar. The 2nd Canadian Corps headquarters was established in the United
Kingdom in January 1943 to command excess Canadian formations not con-
trolled by st Canadian Corps, then destined for the Mediterranean. On becom-
ing active in Normandy during the first week of July 1944, its commander for
the balance of the campaign in northwest Europe was Lieutenant-General Guy
Simonds. The ist Canadian Corps headquarters was transferred back to north-
west Europe from the Italian front in early 1945. Under corps commander
Lieutenant-General Charles Foulkes, it was operational for the final April-May
1945 phase of fighting. 3
