An Assessment of Canadian Formations
THROUGHOUT MOST OF THE Canadian Army's campaign in northwest Europe
from June 1944 to May 1945, all regiment-sized units at the independent brigade,
division, corps, and army level were combat capable. Most units had their full
establishment of personnel, vehicles, and equipment. As a result, all formations,
those organizations of brigade size or larger, were fully capable of offensive ac-
tion for nearly all of their combat deployments. At war's end, each of them had
largely reached their maximum strength and military capability.
The ist Canadian Infantry Division was among the most rested and capable
of the three Canadian infantry divisions as of May 1945. Infantry and other
reinforcements were plentiful, and the number of heavy weapons and "A" vehicles
were sufficient for operational units. There were minor equipment challenges,
but they were limited to the area of heavy armoured fighting vehicles (AFVs)
within the ist Anti-Tank Regiment, Royal Canadian Artillery (RCA). Combat
operations by the division in April 1945 were entirely successful. Any missing
assets, such as the 4th Canadian Reconnaissance Regiment (4th Princess Louise
Dragoon Guards), had their combat power replaced by attached combat arms
units from other formations.' On average, enemy opposition during the last
month of the war was weak. There was no urgent need to address minor short-
ages in the division, as the end of the war was imminent. The division finished
the war adequately equipped in most equipment categories, Like all Canadian
units at this point in the campaign, it was near its authorized strength in
personnel.
