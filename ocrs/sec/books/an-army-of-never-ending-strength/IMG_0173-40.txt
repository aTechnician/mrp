In his seminal 2001 work, Clash of Arms: How the Allies Won in Normandy,
American military historian Russell A. Hart says of the First Canadian Army's
Normandy Campaign: "The rapid but haphazard wartime proliferation of sup-
port and service elements further curtailed the Canadian ability to operate
independently?5! This work would like to respectfully present a different ap-
preciation. The armoured and infantry regiments of the First Canadian Army
closed the Falaise Gap, sealing the fate of thousands of German troops. This
success was due to the fact good service and support services made the combat
arms regiments militarily effective.
The Canadian Army (Active) benefitted from adopting what were largely
British Army practices that had evolved through trial and error over the course
of the war into a very sophisticated system. During the Italian campaign in
1943-44, the army had the opportunity to practise these methods in the field.
On its arrival in northwest Europe, familiar practices were put into action with
excellent results. Canadian combat arms regiments were never hindered in their
operations because of a shortage weapons or vehicles.
