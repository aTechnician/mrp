Artificers and sappers
Labouring work by combed-out rankers was meant to be supervised by
the Royal Engineers, a tiny corps which consisted only of commissione
officers. More productivity was anticipated from supervision by a specialised
tradesmen's corps of Sappers and Miners.
First raised in the siege of
Gibraltar in the 177os, it was officially established in 1787 with six companies
of t0o men. With NOs from the Royal Artillery, each company contained

to be strong able bodied men ... duly qualified for their several trades and
occupations', and an enhanced bounty of 3 guineas was necessary to attract

provided when needed by seconded infantrymen. 102

known as the Stannaries Regiment, which was balloted specifically from tin

Dover. On the latter job, their labourers were supplied by 300 men from the

expanded in i8tt, doubling the numbers of tradesmen. They were supple-
mented by a wide range of new trades recruited from time-served civilians:
bricklayers, braziers, file cutters, gardeners, limeburners, modellers, nailers,
slaters and tilers. By 1813 (when renamed the Corps of Royal Sappers and
Miners), they were nearly 3,000 strong. 103

A major problem arose when in 1802 the martinet Duke of Kent became
Governor of Gibraltar, with a mission to reduce drinking and with drill
and discipline vigorously enforced. The artificers were deprived of the
privilege of working privately in the town, and were once a week taken
from the command of their own officers and drilled and disciplined by
the Town Major. They initially joined a demonstration by the protesting
rank and file of the and and 25th Foot. But meeting separately with their
Engineering officers, the artificers successfully negotiated more working pay,
the removal of canteen restrictions, and 'stated their wish to be drilled by
their own officers'. They stood aside from the rest of the disturbances which
