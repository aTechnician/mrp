the square tower, the chimes echoed over the saltings, breaking
the silence of the dejected, cowering hamlets, carrying far
across the sand-flats towards Tombelaine and the sands of Mont
Nine little girls in white veils and eleven small boys in their
Sunday best took the machine-gun-raked roads which led to the
little church. It stood apart under a blue sky, overlooking
trench-scarred pasture land where refugees from Avranches
rubbed shoulders with German soldiers retreating from the
Cotentin towards Brittany, who avoided the main roads, pre-
ferring to wade fords and cross the meadows.
M. l'abbé Duguépeyroux had wanted to keep his promise—
to deprive the children of Val-Saint-Pére, a seaside village
below the Avranches heights, of their first communion no
longer. The date
cancelled on account of the Landing and finally announced for

This is why the bell "Agatha" tolled from the belfry; it had
been saved from lead requisitioning and been spared the Ger-
man dynamiting and Allied bombing.
"Madness!" exclaimed the countryfolk as they heard the bell
ringing for vespers.
That same morning the neighbouring
church of Ponts-sous-Avranches
had collapsed during the
service; the congregation of ten had survived the bombing,
perhaps by a miracle, or perhaps because they had found
protection beneath the three-centuries-old walls of the sturdy
bell-tower.
