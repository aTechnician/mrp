Midday. Poles and Canadians descending from the heights
occupied that morning, from hedgerow to hedgerow, ditch to
ditch, reached the first houses of deserted Trun. From their
shelter, the refugees on the race course raised the white flag.
Their camp was searched. They progressed cautiously along
the devastated streets in which no enemy was to be seen. The
last Germans had fled at 1000 hours.
"Only slight resistance to the advance of our armour," was
the report that morning of the 4th Armoured Division which,
so that they might cut the Trun-Vimoutiers road during the
day, left the task of taking Trun, now enveloped in a thick pall
of smoke, to the Lincoln and Welland Regiment.
