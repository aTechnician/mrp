"To be accurate, the cauldron isn't yet covered," replied

to cross the Dives between these two points."
A gap three kilometres wide, with a river and two bridges.
One at Saint-Lambert and the other at Chambois. The left bank
of the Dives was held by the Germans, the right by the
Canadians. Beyond, the hills were held by the Poles.
The hills above the plateau towards the Seine fell in fact one
after the other to General Maczek, who had just cut the Trun-
Vimoutiers road. The two German commanders could see these
hills; they promised freedom to all the troops teeming in the
plain, a goal visible to the naked eye. There was no need for
maps, roadsigns or compasses.

from which it is said one has a view of 17 church steeples. To
the right erupted the forest-covered tooth of Mont Ormel from
which several small roads lead to the River Vie.

"What do you think?" asked the C.-in-C. Seventh Army.
"Well, where could we break out except between Trun and
Chambois?" replied Meindl. "Actually we should widen the
gap to the right to ensure Mont Ormel, an exit and an observa-
tion post from where we could literally summon all the units
still in the plain."
Hausser recognized the wisdom of this and approved his
subordinate's plan.
"I suggest that Schimpf with the 3rd Parachute Division
should cross the Dives south of Magny, then bear left for the
northern slopes of Mont Ormel. On his right, Mahlmann with
the 353rd Infantry will cross between Saint-Lambert and
Chambois to take the southern slopes of Mont Ormel."
Meindl outlined in brief the details of the operation.
"Move off from Londe at 2230 hours. No shot to be fired
before dawn. Maximum exploitation of the darkness. Advance
in Indian file. No heavy guns with us. In any case there's no
fuel. The artillery will destroy its guns; if possible a few anti-
tanks and a few 88s will accompany the infantry."
The briefing had hardly finished when "a torrent of shells
and a deafening thunder of guns, which drowned the rattle of
the nearby machine-guns" swept over the plain. Two hundred
1055, 155s and 220s in position at Saint-Loyer, Marcey, Boissey
and Medavi, bombarded the two banks of the Dives and did not
stop for 57 hours.
