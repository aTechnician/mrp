After the stalemate of Operation Epsom, General Montgomery planned a new
offensive to capture Caen in early July. At SHAEF headquarters, Eisenhower was
urging him onwards. Air Marshal Arthur Coningham, a New Zealand air veteran of
the Mediterranean and commander of the 2nd Tactical Air Force, was a vocal critic
of Montgomery, especially of his failure to advance beyond Caen, which in turn had
constrained his squadrons from getting established in Normandy. Montgomery
shrugged off his opponents and assured Eisenhower that he was making progress.
He even boldly claimed that the stalemate around Caen was part of his plan-that
he was drawing the German strength to his front to allow the Americans to break out
of the Cotentin Peninsula. Even though memories were short, most of Montgomery's
detractors well remembered his other promises that he would make much deeper
roads into Normandy before pulling the German forces towards his divisions in
