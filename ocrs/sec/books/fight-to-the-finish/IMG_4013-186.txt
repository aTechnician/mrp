Everyone in the firezone had frequent brushes with death. Captain Joseph
Greenbatt, a doctor with the Royal Canadian Army Medical Corps, said to his wife
on October 12, 1944, "The last five days ... have been absolute hell on earth, and I
do not think I have ever had such a harrowing and nerve-wracking experience before
in this war. I can honestly say that I have never been more scared in my life...
Perhaps in a couple of weeks time l'l tell you all about it, but in the meantime l'l
lock it in securely?"* Like most soldiers, Greenblatt buried his worry, but over time
the vaults of the mind could not hold back the flood of experiences.

Most of the infantry at the front calculated their chances in the life lottery.
Regardless of his level of combat experience, every man assumed that in the end a
shel, bomb, or bullet with his name on it was going to get him one way or the other.
