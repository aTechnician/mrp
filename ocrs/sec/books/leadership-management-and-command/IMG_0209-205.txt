Bradley now had three assault waves bunched up behind the initial one
and he had a further 25,000 troops and 4,400 vehicles waiting.
Here indeed was a crisis in search of a decisive commander; in this case
the metaphorical cavalry arrived, not on waves of horses but the backs of
the waves: the destroyers were moving in. The destroyers standing off
shore had been ordered to cease firing directly on the beach as soon as
the first waves landed at 0630 until ship to shore communications was
established. But 75 per cent of the 116th RCT's radios had been lost in
the water. But by 0810 it was clear to some destroyer captains that the
situation on the beaches was intolerable. The destroyers began shelling
identifiable targets and were then ordered by Captain Sanders,
commander of the destroyer group on the USS Frankford, to move
closer still. His order was reinforced by a message from Admiral Bryant
on the USS Texas. 'Get on them men, get on them. We must knock out
those guns. They are raising hell with the men on the beach, and we can't
have anymore of that. We must stop it', 195
