Not one to remain silent, Moses continued to express his strong views in
September. Writing Bradley again to express his dissatisfaction with COMZ
and the command arrangements, he began by apologizing for the lengthy note,
but claimed a need to get the matter "off his chest in writing." He observed
that the logistical situation had not functioned satisfactorily for the last month
and that he could not understand why the COMZ, that is, Lee, could not fulfill
its responsibility to supply the forces in the field. Moreover, Moses stated he
"felt justified in complaining about the poor service."
Lee's movement of
COMZ Headquarters to Paris, just three weeks after arriving on the Continent,
particularly pained Moses, who saw the move as unnecessary and a waste of
limited transport assets and a disruption of the logistical system as combat forces
suffered shortages of gasoline. Moses concluded by urging Bradley to take up
the COMZ matter with Eisenhower.20
The chaos caused by the COMZ Headquarters move was illustrated in the
experience of Brigadier General James Stration, ETOUSA/COMZ G-4, who
flew to Valognes on September 9 to find that the COMZ Headquarters had
moved to Paris. Only two members of the COMZ G-4 section remained and
"no suitable communications had yet been established for the operation of the
COMZ Headquarters Paris." Moreover, COMZ had made no arrangements to
train Normandy Base Section personnel in supply procedures to take over when
the headquarters left. General Stratton ordered the two remaining COMZ G-4
officers to stay behind and train the replacements sufficiently to "obviate all
possibility of error."21
