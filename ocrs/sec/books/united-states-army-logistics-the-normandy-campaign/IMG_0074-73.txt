Whereas Cherbourg could supply a

Although supply operations at the invasion beaches and minor ports in
Normandy had begun as scheduled, unfortunately for COMZ, VII Corps troops
did not capture Cherbourg, the only major port in Normandy and crucial to the
American supply effort, until nearly two weeks behind schedule.
When the
heavily damaged port handled its first cargo on July 16, only 2,000 tons were
moved, but by late August engineers had the port discharging up to 12,000 tons
daily.

When fall came and inclement weather halted beach operations, the failure to
develop the Brittany ports, delays in getting Antwerp into operation, and Paris's
daily need of 1500 tons led American planners to search desperately for
additional port capacity? COMZ developed limited substitute ports to replace
discharge capacity lost when Eisenhower and Bradley scrapped Operation
CHASTITY, and by November 10, an average of 9,900 tons of cargo per day
was being landed at Le Havre, Rouen, and Morlaix. These supplies came at a
time when daily OMAHA and UTAH beach capacity had dropped to 1,825 total
tons. When inclement weather closed both beaches in November, the opening
of additional ports enabled the Americans to maintain an average discharge
capacity of approximately 25,000 tons a day.Â®
While the opening of the port of Antwerp solved the American supply
problems, it was much longer in coming than expected. Although British forces
captured the port of Antwerp itself in early September, the Scheldt Estuary and
the seaward approaches to the city remained in German hands until mid-
November.' In October and early November, the Allied armies directed their
main effort to clearing the Scheldt Estuary and preparing the port for use.
After it opened in early December, Antwerp contributed an average 14,300 tons
a day during its first month in operation.
While its capacity increased
throughout early 1945, peaking at an average 20,000 tons a day in April 1945,
port operations did not impact operations until long after the pursuit across
France had ended."
