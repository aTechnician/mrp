As has been
seen, the port situation remained critical until Antwerp commenced operations.
A study by the COMZ G-4 plans branch dated August 18 concluded that bad
weather would limit beach unloading after October 1, which meant that the
Americans needed another 51,000 tons of cargo capacity as an offset. The only
solution appeared to be immediately opening the Brittany ports and completing
the Quiberon Bay project.
Moreover, a British need for five to ten thousand
tons of Cherbourg's capacity would further reduce supplies available to the
Americans. Unless additional unloading facilities were acquired, the Americans
would face serious supply shortages. 3s
On August 25, another port report from COMZ Headquarters concluded that
since the development of the southern Brittany coast did not seem likely and the
Americans still needed new facilities, the north coast Brittany ports would have
to be made operational. Ports slated for use included Granville on the south
coast, Morlaix, St. Brieuc, Cancalc, and St. Malo on the north coast (see Table
5-1). 56
Little came from these recommendations. The port of Morlaix, as per the
original plan, opened September 5.
The opening of the other ports did not
occur as expected. Granville became strictly a coal port as did St. Brieuc,
although planners originally intended both to discharge stores as well. Cancalc,
not included in the original plan, never became operational, since COMZ
abandoned it when they decided to concentrate on Le Havre and Rouen further
north, and to increase the capacity of Cherbourg.$
