On June 5, the day before D-day, the invasion assault
forces sailed from south English ports, where they had for
some time previously been gathering,

To keep the convoys from getting lost, at every important
change of course a reference vessel was posted, minesweepers
dropped lighted buoys on the outer limits of channels that
were swept along the English coast, and the convoys were
guided by other navigational aids to their rendezvous area.
From there, the five assault forces turned south to begin
their voyage across to France. Five swept channels, one for
each assault force with its naval escort, led from Area "Z"
to a point farther south where each channel was divided into
a "slow" lane and a "fast" lane. Along the slow lane moved
the LT's, while the big transports and warships sailed along
the other until they all reached a point about eight to 13 miles
oft the coast of France, called the transport area, which was
selected for its distance from enemy coastal defense batteries.
