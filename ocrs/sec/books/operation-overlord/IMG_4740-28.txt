Invasion: Strategy and Techniques
Planning a Landing at Normandy
The "Overlord" invasion plan called for an assault on the
beaches of Normandy. Other beaches facing England had,
by a process of elimination, been rejected as an invasion
route. The beaches in the Pas-de-Calais, pre-invasion studies
showed, had the highest capacity for passing vehicles and
supplies inland, but were located in the most heavily fortified
stretch of enemy-held coast and too close to the main Ger-
man reserves and air fighter forces. The Normandy area, on
the other hand, though somewhat less desirable from the
point of view of the capacity of the beaches and the distance
from England (some 100 miles as against the Pas-de-Calais'
18 to 20) was held by the Germans in comparative weakness.
Inland, the terrain was suitable for the development of air
strips and airfields, a requirement that was considered essen-
tial within the very first days of the landings. Enemy fighter
bases within striking range of the landings were scanty. There
were no substantial reserves of ground forces in the immedi-
ate area. It was stipulated in the "Overlord" plan that the
early tactical objectives of the invasion would be the con-
solidation of a reasonably large beachhead and the capture,
from the land side, of the port of Cherbourg and the ports
in Brittany.
The invasion was conceived in several phases. The pre-
liminary phase began in 1943 with an accelerated bomber
offensive against strategic targets-aimed at weakening Ger-
man resistance.
