Chapter 14
AFTERTHOUGHT
A review of the war in western Europe brings us to the
conclusion that
the strategy, as directed by the Combined
Chiefs of Staff was, in its main particulars, wise and correct.
The main campaigns that were undertaken at various times
during the war corresponded with the relative power rela-
tions of the principal antagonists. Thus, military operations in
the Mediterranean—the saving of the Middle East and the
invasion of North Africa-accorded with Allied capabilities
in 1942, and the wisdom of that strategy was fully vindicated
by events.
A number of benefits
flowed from the Mediterranean
operations: The Germans were deprived of the strategic
offensive; they then lost the whole North African seaboard,
and their sense of security from invasion through southern
Europe was broken; their manpower resources were stretched
to the limit; and their defensive perimeter was considerably
lengthened.
To the Allies it meant a step nearer to final
victory.
The invasions of Sicily and Italy in 1943 followed as a
logical sequence to the victories in Africa. The decision to
mount these invasions was of high strategic soundness. The
campaign in Italy needed only to have been pursued with
greater vigor, especially after the capture of Rome in June
1944, in order to exploit its possibilities in south-central
Europe. A broader political and military vision was called
