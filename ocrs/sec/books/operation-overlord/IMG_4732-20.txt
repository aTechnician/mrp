Grave News from the Middle East
Until the time of the Washington Conference in June 1942,
the English Channel versus Mediterranean Sea controversy
was, to some extent, conducted in an academic atmosphere.
With the British Middle East offensive proceeding rather
successfully, the Western Allies were left a free choice in
the selection of an area for a strategic
counteroffensive.
Even though an invasion of France in 1942 had to be ruled
out, there existed, it could be argued on some grounds, no
urgency in conducting a counteroffensive in the Mediter-
ranean. But as the discussions were in progress, grave news
reached Washington. The British in Libya had suffered
an
unexpected and severe defeat at the hands of Rommel's
Afrika Korps, and Tobruk, which had withstood siege for
well nigh eight months, had fallen. The Eighth British Army
was in retreat along the entire front, falling back on the Nile.
The Allied position in the Middle East had taken a grave
turn.
Not only were
British
Middle East resources-in
themselves very valuable to the Allied cause-
endangered,
but a flanking threat was created to the Russian armies in
the Caucasus and on the Volga.

Allied supply route
through the Persian Gulf to Russia would be cut. The Ger-

Roosevelt and the African Invasion
President Roosevelt favored a North African invasion.
He favored it for two reasons: With the Atlantic and Medi-
terranean seaboards of Africa in Allied hands the Western
Hemisphere, and more especially the Panama Canal, would
be secure and the Middle East oil resources and Persian
Gulf supply
route to Russia saved. The President's own
strategic predilections had all along inclined to a coordinated
Allied countermove
to German-Italian ambitions in the
Mediterranean. And in view of the impossibility of an in-
vasion of France in 1942 and the turn of events in Libya,
his attitude became more fixed in the face of objections to
Mediterranean
operations on the
part of the responsible
Army authorities in the United States, who were backed by
the Secretary of War and Harry Hopkins, the President's
intimate friend.41 The Army attitude is perhaps best stated

they saw "no earthly reason" why the United States should
