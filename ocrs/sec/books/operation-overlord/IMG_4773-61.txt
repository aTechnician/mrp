Artillery Ammunition
Estimates of ammunition requirements called for the liberal
use of artillery shells, since both General Bradley and Gen-
eral Montgomery were proponents of the heavy concentra-
tion of field artillery in support of infantry. General Bradley
had impressed his own army group planners with the idea
that there was to be "no skimping" on artillery ammunition
_"or the Allies," as he put it,
"would have to pay for it
in lives. "26 General Montgomery had demonstrated the im-
portance he attached to the role of artillery during the battle
of El Alamein, which he conducted.
In the case of the American combat units, a shortage
existed in artillery shells both just prior to the invasion and
during "Overlord" itself. The shortage was particularly felt
in shells for the 155-mm and 8-inch howitzers, the weapons
most widely employed in direct support of infantry. Some
of the ammunition suffered, in addition, from certain de-
fects.?7 Particularly disconcerting was the range performance
of the same type shell manufactured by different firms: They
did not fire at uniform ranges. Before they could effectively
be used in combat they had to be tested, for otherwise the
danger existed of firing into friendly troops or not register-
ing on the target.?8 Since similar difficulties had been experi-

Antiaircraft Defense
A prominent place was given by Allied planners to the
antiaircraft defense of important invasion embarkation ports.
Concentration of the available antiaircraft guns was based
on the relative importance of the port or installation area.
To the more permanent static defenses operated by Britain's
Antiaircraft Command were added the mobile antiaircraft
units of both 21 Army Group and the First United States
Army Group until their departure for France. The impor-
tant Portsmouth-Southampton and Portland-Weymouth areas
