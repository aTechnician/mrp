out as an international city, to be occupied and governed by
the United States, Britain, and the Soviet Union collectively.
Of the liberated countries, France and Luxembourg were
intended for United States occupation and Belgium, Holland,
and Denmark for occupation by Great Britain. Norway was
to be occupied by a joint British-American force.

The domestic situation which the Allied planners expected
to find in western Europe was also considered not bright. The
civilian populations, it was thought, would at first welcome
the Allied troops enthusiastically and would cooperate whole-
heartedly with them. Later, however, friction and unrest
would arise unless the needs of the population for food, cloth-
ing, and shelter could be met without delay. After the first
flush of liberation, local politics and contending social move-
ments were considered likely to make themselves heard, com-
plicating the situation facing the Allied occupation author-
ities.9
