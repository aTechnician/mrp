The function of the Build-up Control Organization was to
allot ships and landing craft to specific units embarking for
France and to keep the ground force commanders advised
of the availability of ships so that they could plan the move-
ment of their troops to the assembly areas. It controlled the
cross-Channel movement of troops through the Movement
Control Organization and the turn-around of vessels through
the Turn-round Control Organization.20
The Control Tug Organization served as an agency for
regulating the cross-Channel movement of tugs employed in
towing barges, the individual parts of the artificial harbors,
and other nonautomotive craft. The Control Repair Organi-
zation was responsible for the economical
and equitable
distribution of repair and salvage work. In each of the im-
portant naval ports, such as Portsmouth, Plymouth, Rosyth,
and others, the Admiralty had functioning repair and salvage

Air Supply
Emergency supply by air was prepared for, in case of
the destruction of one or both of the artificial harbors by
enemy aircraft. Plans called for a daily air lift of 1,000 tons
of supplies from D-day to D+14, to be increased to a daily
lift of 3,000 tons by D+30. Certain airfields in the
United
Kingdom were specifically designated from which the sup-
plies, such as rations, motor fuel, and ammunition, would
be flown. Special air strips in Normandy were to be con-
structed to receive them and airborne engineer aviation bat-
talions flown in at the earliest possible moment after the
landings in France to construct the necessary landing strips.24
