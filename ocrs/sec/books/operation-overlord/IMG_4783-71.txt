DECEPTION
A scheme to deceive the Germans of the Allies' true
strategic intentions formed, as we have seen, an integral
part of "Overlord." A stratagem was devised,
designed to
deceive the Germans as to precisely when and where the
main invasion effort would take place.'
The "Fortitude" Plan
Several deceptive projects were considered by the planners,
such as sham invasion threats to Norway, the Bay of Biscay
coast, and the Pas-de-Calais. The purpose of these threats
was to force the Germans to retain their defensive troop
dispositions in western Europe exactly where they were, by
providing them with evidence that the invasion preparations
opposite Normandy were not the only preparations. Since
the threats had to be made to appear plausible, "Overlord"
planners decided not to maintain a threat to the Bay of Bis-
cay coast of France, owing to a shortage of aircraft carriers
to provide close support to assault forces. Efforts were in-
stead concentrated on feigned threats to Norway and
the
Pas-de-Calais, and more particularly to the latter, because
it was in that area that their own estimate of Allied inten-
tions led the Germans to conclude that an invasion would
take place. The Western Allies were favored in their threats
to widely separated areas of the Continent by the possession
of sea power, which provided them with interior lines
communication in relation to western Europe.
