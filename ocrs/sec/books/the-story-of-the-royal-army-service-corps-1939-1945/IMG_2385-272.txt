The Ration Situation, Autumn, 1940
In the autumn of 1940 there was growing evidence that the service
ration was to come under close scrutiny again.
The Government
were faced with the continued and heavy sinkings by U-boats and
surface raiders.
These losses were reducing imports to a serious
degree; furthermore, the continued bombing of London and other
towns had created a situation where the hazards to life of the
civilian seemed little different from those which the soldier at
home might be called on to undergo.
The fact that a soldier
living out of barracks could obtain more highly prized food, such
as meat, than the civilian living in the same house, caused resent-
ment, and to many people there seemed to be little difference
between the energy expended by the factory worker engaged in
long hours on war work and that expended by the soldier under
active training.
