Here Troopers from the Tank Corps, who had already been trained in the overall skills on other tanks
but joined us now to become familiar with the new breed. Sappers in the mean time, with the
assistance of the troopers were being introduced to the art of becoming co-drivers, as well as the
daily and repair tasks associated with the tank. Besides the usual daily tasks such as checking oil and
water there was to be those associated with track and bogie repairs. These are the most vulnerable
from anti-tank mines rendering the tank completely immobile, and 'a sitting duck. I guess there was
some initial animosity but we all soon realized that a crew was a team and requiring everyone to pull
his weight to get the team through the tasks ahead. A 'bone' of contention was they where not going
to forfeit their black berets.
The Churchill tanks that were part of a conventional armoured squadron at that time had the normal
guns of various calibers fitted to them. But as far as we went at this stage and for some time after
our main additional training AVRE crews had placement training of packaged explosive; either the
General Wade or the Beehive at this time. This absence of a device in the turret mantle led to tales
of the yet to be revealed secret weapons and what they might be installed in the AVRE.
My most vivid recollection was learning to drive the beast; it had a crash gearbox and you had to
watch the tachometer to ensure you had the correct engine revolutions before you dipped the clutch
and engaged the next gear when increasing your cruising speed. Alternatively you double de-
clutched as you lowered the cruising speed, or if you had to engage a lower gear, still watching the
tachometer. While learning to drive these monsters, well they were then; we came to love them
knowing our lives would depend on them later; the infantry also became to appreciate them.
