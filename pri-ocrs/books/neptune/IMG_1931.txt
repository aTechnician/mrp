Marshall knew better. He saw clearly that once the Western Allies became
fully committed, the momentum of events and the logistical realities of
attempting to assemble, mount, and execute a large-scale amphibious
assault in Africa would make a spring invasion of France in 1943 impos-
sible. Bowing to reality, however, he acknowledged that the pressures of the
moment, both political and logistical, compelled the decision for Torch.
Time was running out. It was past midsummer, and the Germans had begun
a two-pronged drive, code-named Case Blue, aimed at the capture of both
the Baku oil fields and Stalin's namesake city, Stalingrad. Something had to
be done, and Torch was the only thing that seemed doable. On July 30,
Roosevelt formally announced that "as Commander-in-Chief, [he] had
made the decision." The invasion of North Africa was now to be America's
